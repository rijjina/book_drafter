# Editorial Standards

## Whole-Work Structure

Use only the components required by the governing standard, but plan for:

- cover, title page, copyright/bibliographic page, preface, acknowledgements or explanatory note;
- author/editor information;
- table of contents and, when needed, lists of tables, figures, symbols, and abbreviations;
- main chapters;
- appendices and glossary when needed;
- bibliography or references;
- Thai and English index for a formal ตำรา/หนังสือ when required.

For a formal textbook, the Mahidol manual marks cover, spine, title page, copyright page, preface, author list, contents, main content, bilingual index, and back cover as standard elements. Apply this only when compatible with the governing institution or publisher.

## Chapter Architecture

Keep chapter structures predictable while allowing discipline-specific sections:

1. chapter number and Thai/English title when required;
2. author name for multi-author works;
3. chapter purpose, scope, learning objectives, or reader outcomes;
4. roadmap or key topics;
5. introduction and prerequisite concepts;
6. main content in a logical hierarchy;
7. analysis, synthesis, comparison, application, and an identifiable scholarly contribution expressed through the argument and evidence;
8. examples, cases, figures, and tables;
9. chapter summary;
10. review questions, exercises, or assessment for teaching-oriented works;
11. chapter references or a consistent whole-book reference system.

Use no more heading levels than needed. Keep the hierarchy and numbering consistent across chapters.

## Content Quality

- Keep content correct, current, complete, coherent, and proportionate to the title.
- Explain why a concept matters, how it works, and where it applies.
- Integrate Thai evidence and relevant author research or experience when it strengthens the argument; do not force self-citation or foreground ownership in the prose.
- Express analysis and synthesis in an impersonal academic voice by default. Avoid headings or stock phrases that announce `the author's synthesis`, `the author's view`, or `the author proposes` when the contribution can be shown through evidence selection, comparison, limitations, and a defensible conclusion.
- Introduce a study through its problem, method, evidence level, or result and cite it normally. Use explicit first-person or author-position language only for a required author note or a genuinely personal professional account.
- Avoid compilation, near-translation, fragmented chapter collections, and old theory without current context.
- Maintain a visible central concept and relationships among chapters.
- Do not inflate page count through unnecessary images, large fonts, or repetitive exposition.

## Thai Language

- Use formal Thai, clear subjects and verbs, and moderate sentence length.
- Avoid spoken language, filler, unnecessary pronouns, and circular prose.
- Use Thai terms when established and understandable. Add an English term at first use in each chapter only when it improves precision.
- Define standard abbreviations at first use in each chapter; avoid invented abbreviations.
- Keep terminology, transliteration, names, units, punctuation, and capitalization consistent throughout.
- Use `เช่น` for a partial set and `ได้แก่` for a complete set.

## Figures

- Number every figure by chapter and sequence, such as `ภาพที่ 2.3`.
- Place a title/caption below the figure, cite it in the text, and identify the source.
- State the creator for original figures.
- Obtain and record permission for copyrighted, adapted, patient, radiology, pathology, or identifiable-person images as required.
- Follow Creative Commons conditions exactly.
- Never claim a redrawn copy is original merely because its appearance changed.

## Tables

- Prefer author-constructed tables that synthesize information.
- Number every table by chapter and sequence and place the title above it.
- Put source, adaptation, permission, abbreviation, and note information below it.
- Do not translate or lightly rearrange a source table and call it original.
- Mention each table in the text and explain its instructional purpose.

## Citations And References

- Select one citation style required by the institution or discipline and use it consistently.
- Cite every non-original claim, quotation, dataset, figure, and table.
- Prefer current primary or authoritative sources while retaining seminal sources where justified.
- Include relevant Thai scholarship and author research where appropriate.
- Check every in-text citation against the reference list and every reference-list item against actual use.
- Verify authors, titles, years, editions, journal data, pages, URLs, DOI, and access dates.

## Index And Navigation

For a formal ตำรา/หนังสือ, plan the index during drafting and finalize it after pagination. The Mahidol guide recommends Thai and English terms with at least two levels. Include only meaningful access points, not every repeated word.
